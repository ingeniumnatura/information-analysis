MEASURE SPACES


A measure space consists of three components (K, 𝒜, μ) where:


K is a set,
𝒜 is a σ-ring constructed from a collection of subsets of K, and
μ is a measure, a function with the following properties:

μ(∅) = 0,

for any set A in 𝒜, μ(A) is non-negative:
μ(A) ≥ 0 ∀ A ∈ 𝒜, and

μ is countably additive:
μ({Aₙ | n ∈ ℕ}) = Σ{μ(Aₙ) | n ∈ ℕ, Aᵢ ∩ Aⱼ = ∅ ∀ i≠j}.

μ is countably additive if for any countable sequence {Aₙ} of pairwise disjoint sets in 𝒜 (sets with no common elements), the measure of their union is equal to the sum of their individual measures. Note that while Aₙ is necessarily countable, the set K and σ-ring 𝒜 may be uncountable.


Henri Lebesgue is credited with formally introducing this concept of measure as a generalized notion of "size" or "extent" of a set. For another example, Lebesgue measures applied to 1, 2, and 3 dimensional Euclidean spaces are length, area, and volume respectively.[3]



[3] Remarques sur les théories de la mesure et de l'intégration. Henri Lebesgue. Annales scientifiques de l'École Normale Supérieure, Serie 3, Volume 35, pages 191-250. Publié 1918.
